# AI Pedagogy Protocol — Three-Mode Learning Framework

> This file defines how AI is integrated into every course session. It is research-backed, session-level, and designed so every class has a clear AI posture — even if that posture is "no AI today."

---

## The Three Modes

Every session operates in one of three AI modes. The mode is set in the course planning Excel and appears in the class prep reference and Canvas pages.

### Mode 1: Solo (Build the Muscle)

**What it means:** Students work without AI tools. The goal is to build foundational reasoning, develop personal frameworks, and practice struggling productively with hard problems.

**When to use:** Early-stage concept introduction, case analysis where the thinking process matters more than the answer, exams, quizzes, foundational skill-building.

**Why it matters:** Research on desirable difficulties (Bjork & Bjork, 2011) shows that productive struggle — effortful retrieval, spaced practice, interleaving — produces stronger long-term learning than frictionless performance. If students always have AI assistance, they risk cognitive offloading: outsourcing the thinking that builds expertise. Solo mode protects the struggle that builds the muscle.

**Bloom's alignment:** Primarily targets Remember, Understand, and Apply — the foundational layers where students need to build their own mental models before AI can usefully augment them.

**Instructor moves:**
- Frame it explicitly: "Today is a Solo day — no AI tools. Here's why..."
- Design activities that reward the thinking process, not just the output
- Use cold calls, think-pair-share, and individual written reflection
- Quizzes and in-class writing are natural Solo activities

---

### Mode 2: AI-Partnered (Learn WITH AI)

**What it means:** Students use AI tools deliberately as thinking partners, not answer machines. The goal is to develop AI fluency — knowing when, how, and why to bring AI into intellectual work.

**When to use:** Complex analysis, synthesis tasks, creative problem-solving, strategic recommendations, group projects, research. Best after students have built foundational understanding in Solo mode.

**Why it matters:** Mollick & Mollick (2023) identify seven productive AI roles in education. The key insight is that AI is most valuable not when it replaces student thinking but when it extends it — acting as a sparring partner, simulator, or coach that pushes students beyond what they could do alone.

**Bloom's alignment:** Primarily targets Analyze, Evaluate, and Create — the higher-order layers where AI augments rather than replaces human judgment.

**The Seven AI Roles (Mollick & Mollick, 2023):**

| Role | What it does | Example use |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **AI-Tutor** | Explains concepts, answers questions, provides personalized instruction | "Explain Porter's Five Forces using my industry as an example" |
| **AI-Coach** | Gives feedback on student work, suggests improvements | "Review my VRIO analysis and tell me what I'm missing" |
| **AI-Mentor** | Provides guidance, asks Socratic questions, helps navigate ambiguity | "I'm stuck on my capstone — help me think through my options without giving me the answer" |
| **AI-Teammate** | Collaborates on tasks, divides work, contributes ideas | "Let's brainstorm 10 disruption scenarios for this industry" |
| **AI-Tool** | Performs specific tasks: data analysis, formatting, research | "Summarize the key financials from this 10-K filing" |
| **AI-Simulator** | Creates scenarios, role-plays stakeholders, models decisions | "Act as the CEO of a competitor — how would you respond to our strategy?" |
| **AI-Student** | Explains back to the student (teaching to learn), tests understanding | "I'll explain the resource-based view to you. Tell me what I got wrong." |

**Instructor moves:**
- Specify WHICH AI role students should use (don't just say "use AI")
- Provide a structured prompt or prompt scaffold
- Require students to show their AI interaction (screenshots, conversation logs)
- Debrief: "What did AI help you see that you wouldn't have seen alone?"

---

### Mode 3: Reflection (Meta-Learning)

**What it means:** Students reflect on their learning process — with or without AI — and develop metacognitive awareness. The goal is to build the judgment layer: knowing when AI helped, when it hurt, and what they actually learned.

**When to use:** After Solo or AI-Partnered sessions, at mid-quarter checkpoints, during project retrospectives, as part of end-of-class reflections.

**Why it matters:** The AACSB's 2024 guidance on AI literacy emphasizes that students need not just technical AI skills but the ability to evaluate AI's contributions critically. Metacognition research (Flavell, 1979; Schraw & Dennison, 1994) consistently shows that students who reflect on their learning process learn more durably than those who don't. Reflection mode ensures students don't just use AI — they develop judgment about AI.

**Bloom's alignment:** Targets the metacognitive layer that sits above all six Bloom's levels — the ability to monitor, evaluate, and regulate one's own thinking and tool use.

**Instructor moves:**
- Use structured reflection prompts (not open-ended "how did it go?")
- Compare Solo vs AI-Partnered work: "What was different about your analysis when you had AI help?"
- Ask calibration questions: "Where was AI most helpful? Where did it lead you astray?"
- Build a class-level pattern: track where AI helps and where it doesn't across cases

## Session-Level Integration

Every session in the course planning Excel has three AI columns:

| Column | What to fill in |
|--------|----------------|
| **AI Mode** | `Solo`, `AI-Partnered`, or `Reflection` (one per session) |
| **AI Role** | For AI-Partnered sessions: which Mollick role (Tutor, Coach, Mentor, Teammate, Tool, Simulator, Student). Leave blank for Solo/Reflection. |
| **AI Activity** | Brief description of what students do with AI (or without it). Examples: "Analyze case without AI, compare to AI analysis in debrief" / "Use AI-Simulator to role-play competitor response" / "Written reflection: where did AI help vs. hurt your capstone analysis?" |

### Sequencing Principle

The three modes should follow a deliberate progression within each topic:

1. **Solo first** — build the foundational understanding
2. **AI-Partnered second** — extend and deepen with AI tools
3. **Reflection third** — consolidate and develop judgment

This mirrors Bloom's Taxonomy: build the base (Solo), reach for higher-order thinking (AI-Partnered), then develop metacognition (Reflection). Not every topic needs all three modes, but the sequence should feel intentional, not random.

### Quarter-Level Arc

Across a full quarter, the balance shifts:

- **Weeks 1–3:** Heavily Solo. Students are building foundational vocabulary and frameworks. AI use is limited to specific, structured exercises.
- **Weeks 4–7:** Mixed. Solo for new concepts, AI-Partnered for application and analysis. Reflection checkpoints at midterm.
- **Weeks 8–10:** Heavily AI-Partnered. Students have the foundation to use AI productively. Capstone projects integrate all three modes. Final reflection on AI's role in their learning.

## Research Foundation

This framework draws on four research streams:

1. **Mollick & Mollick (2023), "Assigning AI: Seven Approaches for Students, with Prompts."** Identifies seven productive roles for AI in education, with specific prompt templates for each. The key insight: AI is most valuable when students are told *which role* it should play, not just "use AI."

2. **Desirable Difficulties (Bjork & Bjork, 2011).** Conditions that make learning harder in the short term (effortful retrieval, spacing, interleaving) produce stronger long-term retention. Solo mode protects these conditions; AI-Partnered mode should be designed to add productive difficulty, not remove it.

3. **Cognitive Offloading (Risko & Gilbert, 2016).** When external tools (including AI) handle cognitive tasks, people learn less from the experience. The risk is that students use AI to bypass thinking rather than extend it. The Three-Mode framework addresses this by making the bypass explicit and reflectable.

4. **AACSB AI Literacy Framework (2024).** Business schools must prepare students to work with AI, not just learn about it. The framework requires both technical fluency (using AI tools effectively) and critical judgment (knowing when AI is and isn't appropriate). The Three-Mode approach maps directly: Solo builds judgment baselines, AI-Partnered builds fluency, Reflection builds critical evaluation.

## For the LLM Assistant

When designing a session, check the AI Mode column in the class prep reference:

- **Solo sessions:** Design activities that explicitly exclude AI. Frame the "why" for students. Use quizzes, individual writing, and think-pair-share.
- **AI-Partnered sessions:** Specify the Mollick role. Write a prompt scaffold if one isn't provided. Plan a debrief that surfaces what AI contributed.
- **Reflection sessions:** Prepare structured reflection prompts. Plan for comparison (Solo vs. AI work). Collect patterns for the skill log.

When generating Canvas pages, include the AI mode and any AI activity instructions in the session page so students know the expectation before class.
