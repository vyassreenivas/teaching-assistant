## The one-sentence lesson from Spring 2026

Grading was the dominant, most-repeated, least-automated workload of the quarter, and the memory system only works if it is written every class. Automate grading; run the debrief from Day 1.

## Delivery principles

_Generalizable delivery lessons, not course-specific._

- **Let productive overruns breathe, but pay for them by cutting the next segment, not the recap.** A presentation or discussion that runs 30-45 min long can be the best part of class; protect it, then compress the following block. Never let the overrun eat the closing. (162, Class 3.)
- **One activity per 20-minute content segment, minimum.** Verbose slide-heavy stretches make a session feel heavy. Shorter slide text, more frequent active formats. (162, Class 4.)
- **Always have a recap slide, and never rush the ending.** Close with 2-3 key takeaways spoken aloud, even if it means cutting the prior segment short. Weak endings were the most common self-noted delivery flaw. (162.)
- **Day 2 of a concept block should change modality, not repeat it.** If Day 1 introduced the theory, Day 2 should be student-applied and forward-looking ("apply this to a company in the news"), not a re-lecture. (162, Classes 12-13; Netflix after Gym Wars.)
- **Design so students experience the idea before it is named.** The strongest activities staged the phenomenon first and the theory second (e.g., a disruption game where teams feel the innovator's dilemma before the term is introduced). (162, Gym Wars, Class 12.)
- **Reference, don't replay, long media.** Teardown of a real artifact (a pitch transcript, a video) mapped line-by-line to a framework beats playing the clip; it protects class energy and keeps the framework central. (165, DoorDash teardown.)

## Section management

_Multiple sections, energy differences, time-of-day effects._

- **Evening and smaller sections run lower-energy; compensate with format, not more content.** Adding material makes it worse. Use more active and structured formats instead. (162, Section 3 evening ~19 vs Section 2 afternoon ~30.)
- **Match participation mechanics to group size.** Larger/livelier sections sustain spontaneous-volunteer formats; smaller/quieter sections do better with structured call-outs and think-pair-share than open discussion. (162.)
- **Hold content constant across sections, vary the delivery.** Same concepts and activities, different facilitation. This is the core pattern for teaching two sections of one course (relevant directly to Fall MGMT164's two sections).
- **Track section-level craft separately.** What lands in the afternoon section may flop in the evening one; a single cross-section craft note loses that signal.

## Grading craft

_The highest-leverage automation target. What makes feedback effective and defensible._

- **Calibration is the real work; scoring is fast once it is set.** Every new grading type (quiz, midterm, peer eval, reflection, Part B, finals) needs its own calibration pass. Grade 3-5 submissions first to fix the leniency stance, document that stance in writing, then batch 5-8 at a time, then spot-check the extremes. (162 + 165, both courses.)
- **Validate the gradebook export before trusting it.** Reverse-engineer the Canvas/Camino export's effective weights against the syllabus (it reproduced to within ~0.006 pt in Spring) to catch Current-vs-Final and Posted-vs-Unposted gaps before computing letters. (165, final grades.)
- **Surface every borderline case and name the rule that decides it.** Flag every student within ~0.5 of a cutoff and state the rounding rule that lands them on one side. This is the explicit "instructor's judgment" layer, and it was requested at every single grading session. (165.)
- **Feedback has a reliable 3-part structure: strengths with specific evidence, then gaps with a concrete better answer, then forward guidance.** Never lead with criticism. This held across quizzes, midterms, capstone, and pitches. (162 + 165.)
- **Produce feedback in two formats every time: paste-ready (.md for the LMS comment box) and formatted (.docx for download).** No reformatting step, instructor edits in the .md, final pastes clean. (165, capstone.)
- **Low peer-eval scores hold only with corroboration.** A low mark stands only if multiple independent raters cite the same specific behavior; a lone low rater gets scrutinized, not auto-dropped. Gives defensible grounds, not just a number. (165, peer eval.)
- **Open-prompt and reflection work is gradable at scale with the right key.** Grade against principles + most-likely class examples + correct classification + explicit zero-rules, not one model answer. For reflections, run a liberal "can't be wrong" rubric where deductions become growth notes. (162 midterm make-up; 165 reflections.)
- **"Generous middle" calibration.** Push partial credit toward full where the substance is present without the exact term, but hold firm on misclassification, framework mislabels, and conceptual errors (e.g., CSR-as-CSV). (162.)
- **The grading-ethics test for fairness:** if you could say the accommodation out loud to the whole class without flinching, it is fair. Separates class-fairness from competence-measurement. (165, make-up exam.)
- **Don't reconstruct primary-source quotes or facts from memory; demand the artifact.** Spring shipped a stale-summary factual error and unverified quotes that were only caught when the real transcript was pasted. (165.)

## Student engagement patterns

_What predicts engagement, what kills it, what closes the loop._

- **The close-the-loop move is the highest-leverage teaching act of the term, and it must carry specific falsifiable commitments.** Not "we'll improve quiz clarity" but "discussion questions before every quiz, starting today." Spring honored its commitments and it mattered more than the survey itself. Structure: what's changing / what we're keeping / what's not changing and why. (162 + 165, mid-quarter pulse.)
- **The student-facing climax should sit late, not mid-course.** Treat the big presentation or pitch as the climax, not the midpoint; sequence the term so energy builds toward it. (165 pulse-survey lesson.)
- **Quiz reform toward application was the #1 pulse-survey win.** Rewriting quizzes from recall toward application, dropping the lowest 2, and promising study guidance closed the largest single pain point. (162.)
- **Prioritize team check-ins by structural risk.** Teams facing a binary decision (pivot or commit, pick a market, choose a model) need you more than teams that need encouragement. Triage accordingly. (165, venture teams.)

## Memory and logging discipline

_The meta-lesson, and the thing most likely to break again._

- **Logging discipline did not survive the busy back half, and that cost the most.** Per-class logs and the skill log both went quiet around Class 11 in both courses; the entire back half had to be reconstructed from transcripts. The work happened, the memory didn't. (162 + 165.)
- **The fix is the `debrief` skill, run every class from Day 1.** It already exists and writes the class log, tracker update, skill-log entry, and FERPA sweep in one pass. The only failure mode is not running it. Make the one-line skill-log entry part of the post-class debrief so it cannot be skipped. (165, end-of-term retro.)
- **Tag patterns as template-vs-instance when they emerge, not in a June archaeology pass.** Reusable patterns scattered across class logs and transcripts were expensive to reconstruct. The skill log is supposed to catch them live; keep entries to two sentences so they actually get written.

## Course-design fixes (push these upstream, before the term)

_Lessons that belong in the syllabus or assignment setup, not in the grading skill._

- **Force structured intake forms for anything you will grade.** Free-text peer-eval forms (scores typed as prose, teammates named by nicknames) were the single worst grading friction of the quarter. A numeric field + a dropdown of real roster names removes most of the pipeline's fragility. (165.)
- **One canonical rubric per assignment on disk; delete or clearly mark deprecated ones.** A wrong-rubric mix-up (100-pt combined vs 50-pt BMC-only) forced a mid-grading judgment call. (165.)
- **Decide rounding and grade-spread policy in the syllabus, deliberately.** Spring's distribution came out compressed without an explicit decision to make it so. (165.)
- **Bake the privacy pattern in at bootstrap, never retrofit.** students.json + gitignore + ID-only-on-disk + a leak-check grep must exist before the first name is written. Retroactive de-identification is risky; the safe move is to never write the name to a committable file. (165, where the pattern was copied from 162 a few days late.)

## Sandbox and tooling constraints (bake into every grading skill)

- **The sandbox cannot recalc spreadsheet formulas (no LibreOffice).** Write computed values as static numbers so the file shows correct results on open.
- **Submission discovery must be robust:** recurse the folder tree, catch `LATE`-named files, and re-extract garbled/template PDFs on a second pass. Flat listings under-count.
- **Confirm edits actually landed.** LMS quiz-type churn (Classic vs New Quizzes, Graded Quiz vs Graded Survey, essays auto-scoring 0 until full-credited in SpeedGrader) caused real misses; keep an LMS setup playbook of the gotchas and verify each requested edit applied.
- **Prefer propose → confirm → atomic Python write for tracker mutations,** with a timestamped backup before any structural change, and a print summary of before/after counts. More reliable than Edit-tool calls on large JSON.

## Batching and triage heuristics

- **Batch routine status flips; handle substantive changes one at a time.** Ten overdue-item resolutions in a single proposal-plus-atomic-write beat ten separate confirmations. (165.)
- **Triage before action on any tracker untouched for 24+ hours.** Ask "what's already done?" first; the tracker lags reality. A single multi-question triage call caught that 10 "overdue" items were already complete. (165.)
- **Batch-produce all per-team materials in one session** (team plans, evaluation sheets, feedback) rather than spreading them across sessions. (165, pitch sprint.)

## Session-prep input checklist (run before designing any session)

Answering these six before building a session plan produced reliably better plans (165): (1) What concept? (2) What case or example? (3) What is the survey/feedback signal right now? (4) What happened last class? (5) What are the teams/students currently working on? (6) Any logistics (grades to return, announcements, schedule changes)? A "session prep context bundle" that auto-assembles these is a known, wanted enhancement.
